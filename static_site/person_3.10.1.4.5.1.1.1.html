EDWIN HOYT,

ID: 3.10.1.4.5.1.1.1 | Source: Joseph
(3.10.1.4.5.1.1.1) EDWIN HOYT, Jr. (#5905) born May 29, 1838 Castleton VT; served 3y 2m in the 2d Michigan Cavalry Regiment (Civil War), which was organized at Detroit MI October 2, 1861. The Regiment left MI for St. Louis MO, November 14th; duty at Benton Barracks MO till February 21, 1862; very long list of campaigns; if interested, check out the Compendium of the War of the Rebellion, which lists them all. Edwin was promoted to First Lieutenant and Adjutant; served on the staffs of General Samuel Carter, Colonel A. P. Campbell, and General J. T. Croxton; engaged in battles before Cornith, and at Chaplin Hills or Perrysville; took a rebel flag at Elizabethtown KY; was with Carter’s Raid across the Cumberland Mountains; engaged at Chickamauga, Dandridge TN; Franklin TN, Pulaski, Florence and many others; mustered out December 7, 1864; married April 14, 1864 Helen Maria Knight. Edwin was a merchant and lived in Detroit MI. (3.10.1.4.5.1.1.2) BIRNEY HOYT (#5906) born October 13, 1841 Sinclairsville, Chautauque, NY; in 1845 his parents moved to Farmington MI; 1852 to Detroit, and in 1857 to Grand Rapids; attended the public schools; served 3y in the 6th Michigan Cavalry Regiment (Civil War), which was organized at Grand Rapids MI, from August 28 to October 13, 1862; duty at Grand Rapids until December 10, when the Regiment left the State for Washington, DC; numerous campaigns; see Compendium. He was promoted to Captain [listed as First Lieutenant in unit history]; wounded at Trevillian Station and captured; in Libby Prison for 5 days; in Annapolis Hospital for 6 months; engaged in many of the battles of Virginia, Gettysburg, and Sheridan’s raids on the James, and the engagements just previous to Lee’s surrender, where he was in the front ranks; mustered out June 21, 1865; attended the law department of the University during winter of 1865-6, and was admitted to the bar 1866; married June 18, 1867 Helen Maria Noye. Birney was a lawyer and resided in Grand Rapids MI; practiced law there up to 1871; held the office of city recorder for 4y; elected circuit judge 1871 and reelected 1875. William L. Clements Library, The University of Michigan, Helen Maria Noye Hoyt Papers Helen or Nellie M. Noye was born December 26, 1839 in Rye, New York, but her family lived in Buffalo, N.Y., where her father, John T. Noye, was a clerk. The family stayed in Buffalo, where John Noye later became a wealthy manufacturer of mill machinery. Helen had two brothers Hadie and Richard K., and a sister Lizzie. In August of 1863 Helen journeyed to Annapolis, Maryland, to spend one year as a volunteer nurse in the U.S. General Hospital Division No. 1, also called the Naval Academy Hospital. The hospital had opened in 1857 and remained open through out the Civil War, even though the Naval Academy itself had been removed to Newport, Rhode Island. Helen was the youngest of 10 nurses at the hospital, so young that when Dorothea Dix, chief of the army nurses, visited the camp, Helen was "spirited away" in fear that she would be ordered to leave because of her age. Helen worked under the supervision of nurse Sister Tyler, and the surgeon in charge Dr. B. A. Vanderkeift. She roomed and worked with Maria or Hallie Hall, who was 27 and, of all the nurses, closest in age to Helen. Helen and Miss Hall became "best and life long friends," both of them being very religious and more critical of the other nurses for their exaggerations and drama. Helen's work was mainly to tend to the wounded soldiers. She distributed food to the soldiers, wrote letters they dictated, and she wrote letters to the soldiers' families after their deaths. Her deep religious sentiments lead her to read sermons to the soldiers, comfort them spiritually, and in some cases convert them. When she started working, most of her patients were paroled prisoners from Richmond, Va. Later patients came from those wounded at the battles around Petersburg, and eventually only wounded officers were assigned to the hospital. Helen showed kindness to all the soldiers who came through the hospital, including black soldiers who were segregated, and a young Confederate soldier, who died at the hospital. In early March of 1864, while Miss Hall was away, Helen was given extra duties and placed in charge of section five. In addition to tending more soldiers, she was in charge of arranging their meals. Helen very much enjoyed preparing the soldiers' food, even if she did feel "how hard a lady's position as supervisor is." In July 1864, due to the proximity of the Confederate Army, Helen and the other nurses were ordered to leave the hospital. After departing the hospital, the train on which Helen was riding was captured by the Confederate Army just north of Baltimore. Helen and the other passengers were helped off the train and left with their baggage and a handcar. The next day the abandoned passengers were picked up by a steamer and Helen returned to Buffalo to care for her ailing mother. Helen never returned to the hospital and the work to which she was "heart and soul devoted." In 1867 Helen married Birney Hoyt (1841-1900) in Buffalo, N.Y., and the couple moved to Grand Rapids, Michigan. Birney Hoyt was a lawyer, and from 18711881 a circuit court judge. According to the 1880 census, they had four sons, John, Richard, Hobert, and Haward. A fifth son, Arthur Birney was born in 1880. They also had two female servants. Helen Noye Hoyt outlived her husband by 26 years. She died February 7, 1926.

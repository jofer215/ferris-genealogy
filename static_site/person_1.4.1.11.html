GEORGE FERRIS

ID: 1.4.1.11 | Source: John
(1.4.1.11) GEORGE FERRIS I do not know if this is the same George Ferris, but on February 11, 1792, George Ferris and Edward Ferris were indicted, in New York City Court, for assault on Martin McEvoy. They both plead guilty and were fined 28s. 8d. each. On November 12, 1792, a George Ferris was indicated, in New York City Court, for petty larceny. He was convicted and sentenced to gaol for 15 days. On July 7, 1797, a Samuel Gidney was indicted for assault and battery on George Ferris; he plead not guilty. He was tried on July 8, and found not guilty. From Life in the Bronx, October, 1953. The Bronx has been largely peopled and enriched during the past fifty years by successive tides of immigrants from many lands and we normally think of the average Bronxite as a first or second generation American. Without ever giving it any conscious thought I had always assumed Dick Ferris [Richard S.], of Ferris Buick, Inc., fell in this category and I was both surprised and curious to learn the other day that one of our Bronx streets had been named for his family. Here was a fact of Life in the Bronx worth investigating. I chased over to Ferris Buick to see what could be learned. I was able to unearth the fact that a genealogy of the Ferris family was in the 42d Street Library and from its pages I obtained the story. The Branch of the Ferris family for whom Ferris Avenue and Ferris Place are named stems from John Ferris, who emigrated to Throggs Neck from Fairfield CT, in 1654. He had nine children. During the Revolutionary War, when the British forces, under General Lord Howe invaded Throggs Neck, the house of James, one of the children, was used by them as headquarters. James Ferris was captured by the Queens Rangers during the fighting and subsequently died of the result of the rigors of his captivity. (1.6.1) JOHN FERRIS (III?) (IV?) born 1722/24/27/28 and died April 28, 1805 Cortlandt. John took an active part in the Revolution as a member of the Council of Safety. He lived on the Yorktown Road to Peekskill in Westchester County during the war. He was Commissioner of Confiscated Property. He lost money, grains, barns, and property. His house was burned when Bedford was burnt, July 2, 1779 by LtCol Lancaster Tarleton of British Light Horse. John was Justice of the Peace during the War and made various reports on widows and children and others whose property had been taken by soldiers; seized cargo of boats unloading at Peekskill to prevent supplies reaching the British. Some of his activities in defense of his country are mentioned in the Public Papers of George Clinton, first governor of New York State. He resided in Cortlandt, Dutchess County NY based on the 1790 census with shows himself, wife and two daughters living at home, although information is only available on one ‘possible’ daughter.

SARAH R. RYDER

ID: 1.2.5.4.2.1.5.1 | Source: John
(1.2.5.4.2.1.5.1) SARAH R. RYDER born January 28, 1836 Newcastle Township NY and died October 16, 1923 due to accidental injuries at Meridien CT; buried Dale Cemetery, Ossining NY; married Edward Marsland (Photo) born October 5, 1829 Newcastle Township and died June 27, 1898; buried Dale Cemetery. Edward was educated at the academy in Sing-Sing and spent 4 years at Allaire shipworks, NYC headed by William Webb. He resided in Ossining, NY and was a naval engineer; Constructing engineer and fleet engineer of Russian Fleet in Chinese waters. In August 1860 he resigned his position in Russian Navy to offer his services to the United States Navy. Aug. 14, 1861 appointed 1st assistant engineer in United States Navy. Aug. 23, 1861 appointed first assistant engineer on gunboat Unadilla. While taking this vessel to Hilton Head the engine broke down. He repaired them at sea, and put them in condition to participate in engagement at Port Royal the following November. Naval Engagements in which he participated: Hilton Head; North Edisto; Ossibaw Sound; Stonewall Inlet; Bombing of Fort Pulaski; Blockading of Charleston Harbor and off Florida. Vessel ordered to Brooklyn. Mr. Marsland detached from her; ordered to superintend the construction of the engines of the Lackawanna. Next he was sent out on Lackawanna as chief engineer in the blockade off Mobile; iick with Southern fever; ordered home on sick leave; on special duty at Brooklyn Navy Yard. He resigned just before assassination of President Lincoln. Mr. Marsland was in the navy nearly four years. Next Mr. Marsland was sent as chief engineer of the Colorado, to Pacific coast, as a chief engineer in the Pacific Mail Service." -Cora Marsland letter to VLR(?) May 1939 "Through the influence of William H. Webb, ship builder, he was appointed first assistant engineer of the Italian frigate Re Luigi de Portogallo (that had just been built) on its trip to Italy. In August of 1867, at the insistance of Mr. John Roach, he was engaged to take the ironclad ram, Dunderborg, (which the government of the U.S. had just sold to France) to the port of Cherbourg, France. Mr. Marsland was then for two years the chief engineer of the Essayons dredging the lower Mississipi River. He was then directed by the U.S. government to make drawings for another dredge (the General Mcallister) and to go to New York City to superintend its construction. About this time John Roach bought the iron works at Chester, PA and induced Mr. Marsland to resign from the government engineering corps and become superintending engineer of his establishment. He remained in this position more than three years during which time the City of Sydney and the City of New York were built. Then he became inspector of steamers for the Port of New York. He crossed the Indian Ocean twice, the Atlantic 99 times and the Pacific 5 times. From the Orient and Italy, France and England, he came home laden with treasures of art. Owing to impaired health, he returned to his estate that commanded an unusually beautiful view of the Hudson River. From 1893-1898, the last years of his life, he had his first respite from arduous work and grave responsibility. Of such stuff heroes are made. He died June 1898." - Cora Marsland letter to Sarah Marsland Sammis. Born to them was:

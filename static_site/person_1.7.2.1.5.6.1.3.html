EDWARD LAWRENCE SCHIEFFELIN

ID: 1.7.2.1.5.6.1.3 | Source: John
(1.7.2.1.5.6.1.3) EDWARD LAWRENCE SCHIEFFELIN (Photos) born October 8, 1847 Tioga Co PA and died May 12, 1897 Day’s Creek, Douglas Co OR; Founder of Tombstone AZ; Ed was prospecting in Mohave Co in northwest Arizona in the fall of 1877 when he got the chance to travel to the opposite end of the state with a company of soldiers going to Fort Huachuca in the middle of the bloody Apacheria. A prospector will always go somewhere he’s never been if it means a chance to look over new territory. Ed went. After he arrived at Fort Huachuca, he saw in the color of the surrounding hills something he liked. He was warned that if went out alone, looking for his fortune, all he would find would be his tombstone. Disregarding this warning, Ed set out alone. He was cautious enough never to make a move without searching the area for even the slightest sign of the dreaded Apache. He never had a fire at night and always slept among the boulders on a hillside. In the fall of 1877, Schiefflein struck pay dirt. He knew the ore was rich, so he gathered samples and headed north to the tiny community of Signal after deciding to call his strike The Tombstone. When assayed at Signal, the strike proved to be rich indeed. In the company of his brother Al and the assayer, Richard Gird, Ed headed back to his claim and the men began digging the rich ore. They made their base camp about three miles north of where Tombstone is today. Eventually the Schieffelin brothers sold their interests in the mine and left Tombstone wealthy mean. Ed traveled the world, wore the most expensive clothes, ate at the best restaurants, and was a curiosity to all who met the man who discovered a whole Mountain of Silver. By 1883, Ed was tired of the rich life and headed to Alaska to prospect. He returned in the fall to San Francisco where he married Mary Elizabeth Brown (married 1838 in La Junta CO); a widow from VA, who had a daughter from her first marriage. In 1884, the couple settled in Alameda CA, but the city life was not for Ed and he bought a ranch in Oregon. Ed was found dead in his cabin on May 12, 1897; as per his request his body was sent to Tombstone and he was buried beneath a prospectors mining claim monument with all of his prospecting clothes and gear.

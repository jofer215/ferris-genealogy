CLARENCE CLARK FERRIS

ID: 5.1.3.6.4.9.1 | Source: James
(5.1.3.6.4.9.1) CLARENCE CLARK FERRIS (Photos) born February 15, 1864 Greenwich and died date unk (1961) Scarsdale NY; attended the Claverack Academy, and the Hudson River Institute, Claverack, Columbia Co NY; graduated Yale College, B. A. 1887; graduate Columbia Law School, L.L.B. 1892; admitted to the New York Bar 1891; admitted to the Connecticut Bar 1908; 1887, Clarence was the principal of the public schools of Colchester CT, holding a similar position in Manchester CT, the following year. In his law practice, he specialized in condemnation proceedings and made a success in his chosen field. He took part in civic affairs in his adopted town of Scarsdale NY. He was a Democrat and in 1916, was a candidate for the New York Senate from Westchester County. He was a member of the National Democratic Club of New York and maintained his residence in Scarsdale. Along with his brother, Harry, they owned 56 acres of the original farm granted to their ancestor and that land was leased to the Sound Beach Golf and Country Club. He married, first, January 14, 1897, Katherine Dudek who died October 17, 1905; married, second, April 16, 1908, Bertha Vincent Odell (1877-1969) [dau of Edward Valentine Odell]. Presuming this is the correct fella – Brooklyn Eagle, June 12, 1902: Attorney Clarence C Ferris, who represents several owners of property required for the new Eastern District Bridge, has written to the Bridge Commissioner Lindenthal as follows: “You state that numbers 206 Havenmeyer street, 218, 231, 233 and 239 South Fifth street were offered to the New East River Bridge Commission by the several owners for the various sums which you mention, and that such offers were accepted, and that you are willing to buy all of said parcels at the prices agreed upon between the owners and the New East River Bridge Commission. The several owners have withdrawn and rescinded, through myself as attorney, the several offers mentioned, and have made other offers to sell to the City of New York, as contained in my letter to you. Those are the only offers which are now in existence. I regret that you have formed the opinion that the prices which I have named in my letter on the 3d instant are too high. I beg to call your attention to the fact that they are much more reasonable in every instance than prices which the city has already paid for various parcels of property in the approach and plaza. Owners of property in cases of acquisition of the same for a public purpose always feel, as they do in the case of taxation, that equality is equity. And they wish to be treated as well as others are treated. Obviously, the only treatment which will be satisfactory to property owners is to pay each one the same as any other has been paid when the values are substantially equal. If this is not done the city acquires at a low rate the property of those who are needy while the wealthy are enabled to hold out for full value. I will not consent to see any one of my clients receive any less for his property than any other person has received for a similar property. These people for the the most part are going to be turned out of homes which represent the accumulations of a lifetime, and which are worth much more to them in general than best prices which had been paid for similar property. We do not expect to be allowed for the rents which have been lost for the past five years, or to have the amount of the taxes and water rates which have been paid returned, but we do expect to receive the full market value of the property, as established by the sales already made.” Born to Clarence and Bertha were:

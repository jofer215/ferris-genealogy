Conversation that Abraham S Underhill and Abraham I Underhill had. Memoranda of conversation with Abraham I Underhill in which he gave some account of the Underhill family (of his own times) from recollection: My father (Isaac Underhill) was born on his father’s (Abraham) farm situated near White Plains, Westchester County, N.Y. (Yorktown NY). He was a man of great bodily strength and sound constitution. He was very industrious, even to old age, say, over 80. During he life he partly cleared up three farms near Saw Mill River, Nanahagan or Phillips Manor, now the town of Mount Pleasant. He was a man of good natural ability, but little improved by education, yet was a man of good judgment and was an elder of the Society of Friends for many years. His father (Abraham Underhill) lived at White Plains. Before father left White Plains he partly cleared the farms of his brothers Jacob and Abraham at Nanahagan, he going back and forwards by a footpath, which was the only road between the two places. The neighborhood was then inhabited by Indians and wild animals. He moved to Nanahagan about 1738, at which time the Indians had not yet left the neighborhood. He moved to Amawalk (Yorktown) in 1774. The Amawalk farm was cleared when he bought it, and was one of the first farms cleared in that part of the country. During the Revolutionary War Washington’s main army on its way from White Plains to New Jersey marched past the house, going from Pines Bridge to Peekskill. I frequently saw Washington pass during the time, so I knew as well as I did anybody. He always had a lifeguard of twelve young gentlemen riding before him with drawn swords for protection and honor. They were said to be Virginians. I recollect perfectly well meeting him by the schoolhouse near Parker’s and at another time a little south of the old Amawalk Meeting House, and also when he passed our house the two former times he was not with the Army. When I saw him pass our house it was when he proclaimed that he intended to take New York, and was making every exertion to collect men and stores for that purpose, but his real intention was to divert the British in New York to prevent their sending forces to Yorktown, Virginia, to relieve Lord Cornwallis. Washington went down as far as White Plains and then commenced forced marches to West Point, and so through New Jersey south to Yorktown, Virginia, and succeeded in capturing Cornwallis and all his army before he could be relieved from New York. A sergeant’s guard was kept at our house almost during all the war. Major Andre with lawyer Smith of Orange County, N.Y., called at my father’s the day he was taken, at about 8 o’clock in the morning; they wanted breakfast. Andre was on his way from West Point to New York and made many inquiries respecting the road. His mind appeared to be very much impressed, so that he could not be still a moment. Smith ate breakfast which mother prepared for him, but Andre appeared very agitated, walked backwards and forwards before the front door, and ate nothing. He had on a blue light-bodied coat, made plain after the fashion of the New England farmers of the day. The New England farmers, having manufactories, wore good clothes, but those of this part of the country were unable to obtain good clothes, not having any fulling mills or manufactories. Smith went back north from our house and Andre went towards New York alone. He went by the way of Pines Bridge and Tarrytown. Near the latter place he was taken prisoner by Van Wart and others who were out on a scouting party. In the time of the Revoluntion the British Light Horse came through this part of the country. They came from Crumpond to our house. They burned several houses, killed one or more men and wounded every man they met, taking them off prisoners to New York; they robbed every house and took off all the cattle they could find. A few weeks before a hurricane had passed over our house and took the roof off the back side. The British rode up very furiously with their swords drawn and demanded which party took the roof off the house. I replied, ‘the Upper Party,’ which they understood to mean the Whigs, the British being the lower party. They then placed a sentinel at the door, who prevented any of the party from going into the house or taking anything from us, which we considered as a remarkable Providential preservation. In the Revolution robberies and murders were frequent in this part of the country. One night there were two strangers put up at our house from New England. In the dead of the night a man knocked at the door. Father asked who was there; the reply was a ‘friend.’ Father mistook the voice for that of a particular friend of his, but mother thought they were robbers beyond any doubt. Father got up and opened the upper half of the door and soon found his mistake, for they were robbers who had come to rob the house. Mother jumped out of bed the same time father did. While he was parleying with the man over the lower door the man stood outside with a gun in his hand and his companions at his heels. She woke up all her children, seven sons and four daughters (all as large as men), put men’s coats and hats on the girls, all in not over three minutes, and marched them into the room where father was. So large a company struck such a damper on the robbers, that they immediately retreated. We then looked for the strangers who had put up with us; was a long time before we found them; they were hid away under their bed, backsides near the wall; they were very much frightened, having $1,500 with them (Continental money, which was then good – this was about 1776). They were very thankful for their deliverance. The same robbers had a short time before robbed a tenant house on our premises. It was afterwards found out who the robbers were; they were arrested, tried and punished by putting them on them the chemises they had stolen from out tenant, putting them on horseback and marching them off in that style on the road starting from our farm. I was present when they marched away. One of the robbers was a neighbor and is still living (Sep 1840) in this neighborhood, but from shame has not showed himself out of his house these 30 years. One evening, being detained at work in the fields, on my return towards the house and when a little west of the house, I suddenly (it being dark) found myself stepping on some men and was much frightened. These men (about 100) were lying flat on the ground. Their quarters were at the house now occupied by Joshua Carpenter. An officer stepped up to me and inquired who I was and where I was going. I replied that I lived in the house nearby and had been detained at work, that I was then on my way home. He made me promise to say nothing about what I had seen and then let me go. Their object was apparently to prevent a surprise or to intercept some enemy. Joshua Husen was a notorious character in the Revolution. He was connected with the British Army, though a native of his town (Yorktown). He frequently passed through the American Army acting as a spy and carrying his information to Canada. Rochambeau’s Army lay encamped in Yorktown about six weeks. It had not been there one week before Husen passed through it. He frequently lay in ambush and shot down American officers or others whom he disliked. All efforts to secure him were unsuccessful, though large rewards were offered for his apprehension. At length he with his confederates broke into the house of Seth Whitney (now Barney Montross), abusing and beating him bodily, carried away everything they wanted. Husen told Whitney that he would come again and then would kill him. The old man (Whitney) fixed a bayonet to a rake handle, determined to kill Husen should be come again. At length Husen came once more, but for the last time. Whitney’s wife told Husen he had better not come into the house, for her husband would kill him. But disregarding her, he tried to break in; when about half through the window Whitney stabbed him just below the breastbone, making a wound about one and one-half inches deep. Husen, nothwithstanding, got into the room and beat Whitney until he was thought dead. Husen and his fellows then got on their horses and rode away. At first the family believed Whitney dead, but after a time they were successful in restoring him and he soon got well. When I saw him the day after the beating he was dreadfully bruised. Husen’s would prove much more serious than he at first supposed, for he bled internally, and when within 20 roads of our house he said, “I am a dead man,” and fell from his horse dead. His comrades applied to some Tories in the neighborhood, who took the body and buried it about eighteen inches deep under an apple tree in Veil’s orchard, on the east side of the road. The next day they dug another grave behind the hill in the woods southeast of William Smith’s house, a very out-of-the-way place. To conceal the grave they cut down a tree, making it fall over the grave. Five months afterwards it became generally known that Husen was buried there. The neighbors dug him up and crowds of people came (some from a great distance) to see the body, for he was a terror to the whole country around. They wanted to satisfy themselves that he was really dead. I was proposing to hang the body on a gibbet, but it was partially put back into the grave, where it probably remained undisturbed. Several of the men who first buried him were afterwards discovered to be the same that robbed our tenant house and intended robbing us. Whitney, as a reward for killing Husen, was exempt from taxes during the remainder of his life. This Husen was of the same family as the man who was hanged on a gibbet for instigating the negroes’ insurrection in New York City. Rochambeau, while in our neighborhood, had his headquarters at my father’s house. The generals always dined together, dining alternately at each other’s quarters; once a week they dined at our house. Great preparations were made for the dinner, they having a French cook who had served in he kitched of a French King, and he had ten assistants. Once morning a man came running to our house saying that the party of Americans under Col. Greene, who were quartered at Danford’s (now Joshua Carpenter’s), consisting of 50 men, were all killed or wounded. It appeared the British Light Horse, each rider with another behind him, had surprised the Americans immediately after they had called in the sentinels in the early morning and had killed or wounded them as they lay in their tents, which were picketed around the house. At this time there was a party of Americans stationed at our house commanded by a Lieutenant, but it was probably unknown to the British, or they might have cut them off at the same time. Had they attacked the house the family would have been in great danger, for some of the boys were in bed with the officers, and in the confusion it was scarcely possible that all would have escaped uninjured. I often thought of making the house musket-proof by casing the outside with logs. The house of James Quinby (who married sister Hannah) at Westchester, now Pearsall’s, was often robbed during the Revolution and the family had a place of concealment to which they fled whenever the house was attacked. One night Robert Underhill (my brother), James Underhill and Isaac Mekeel (who afterwards married sister Hannah when a widow) were at James Quinby’s house when Robert proposed defending the house when the robbers came, provided James would assist him. That very night the robbers came, sure enough, but the heart of James failed him and he fled with the rest. Robert, however, provided himself with a large club and arranged the chairs along the entry. Then he commenced a violent attack on them, striking them with his club, stamping and making all the noise he could, as though there were fifty men inside the house. He struck against the door and dared the robbers to come on, saying, “We are ready for you,” etc. He then went upstairs and blew a conch. The robbers fired a volley at the house, but missed him. They then went off; afterwards they reported that they thought the Devil was in the house. The British Army had possession of New York. Their northern lines were at Kings Bridge; the American lines were at Croton River, about 25 miles north of the British lines. The district between was occasionally occupied by each party and by thieves and murderers, but few of the former inhabitants of this district chose to remain at home. There being no cattle to eat the pasture, the grass grew to a great height, so that it fell down and rotted on the ground from year to year. In 1777, we being just above the American lines, the cowboys stole from us 14 head of cattle and drove them to Morrisania. Mother, on horseback, traced them to the British lines. She then determined to appeal to the British generals who were quartered at Harlem Heights. When she arrived at Kings Bridge she met standing as sentinel the same light horseman that was posted sentinel at the door of our house when the British came past after the roof of our house was blown off. He recognized her and she him. He kindly led her horse across the bridge. She gave him a Pistereen (Jim-money) and went on her way to the General’s quarters. She proved to the General’s satisfaction that her husband was one of the people called Quakers, who took no part in the war. He then gave her an order to the cowboys either to return the cattle or pay for them. She went to Morrisania and showed the cowboys the order of the General and demanded the cattle. This put the robbers in a great rage and they threatened to kill her and her family. Mother thought it too dangerous to carry the money home with her through the neutral grounds and concluded to deposit it with Israel Underhill, a particular friend of hers, living at Westchester, who hid it in a stone wall on his farm and gave it back to mother after she reached home in safety. The object of her journey was, however, known to the people in our vicinity and the skinners afterwards came to the house and enquired how she succeeded about the cattle. She told them. They then tried by some kind of agreement to get the gold from her, for they dare not take it by force. Mother told them she had left it at Westchester. They told her she had done well, but was a fool for leaving the money at Westchester. At another time the cowboys stole three horses from us and took them to Morrisania. Mother followed them again on horseback and went to the commanding officer, who compelled the robbers to give them up, so she returned home leading the three horses. Brother Robert had a notion of entering into business and bought a quantity of goods on credit, offering them for sale in the east room (front) of father’s house, brother Joshua being clerk for him. They remainded there about one year. Robert then married, Mary, daughter of Jesse Hallock, and moved his goods to her father’s house on the farm now occupied by William Parker (1848), not far from Hallock’s Mills; a year after he took Joshua and myself into partnership. Joshua being just about of age, we afterwards bought the farm and continued there about six years, carrying on a large business for that time, putting up four or five hundred barrels of pork and beef during a season. Sister Mary kept house for us until she married. In 1792 we took a lease from Van Cortlandt & Co. of the mill site at mouth of Croton River, and built a mill of six run of stone. After the mill was finished Joshua moved to New York to buy wheat for the mill and to sell the flour manufactured from it. Robert and myself lived at the mill to attend the business there. In 1804 Robert sold out his one-third of the mill and bought Tellers Point, to which place he moved his family and lived there until his death. He was an approved minister of the Society of Friends. Tellers Point yet belongs to two of his sons. Joshua continued to reside in New York until his death in 1839. The lease of the mill site expired in 1813. The improvements were appraised, each party selecting one appraiser and the two a third, namely: Van Cortlandt, a farmer by name Anderson; the Underhills, Samuel Mott, large flour merchant and miller in New York, and these to selected David Lydig, the large dealer in flour and most extensive mill owner in the city. Their appraisement the Van Cortlandts refused to abide by. A suit was commenced against them, which was not finally settled until 25 years had elapsed and all the original parties were dead. I removed to Amawalk and resided on the old homestead until I bought the farm adjoining belonging to the estate of my brother Moses, and on which I now live.

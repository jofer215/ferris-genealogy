ROBERT LIVINGSTON PELL

ID: 1.4.7.3.2.2 | Source: John
(1.4.7.3.2.2) ROBERT LIVINGSTON PELL (1st born?) born May 10, 1811 and died February 13, 1880; graduated from Harvard and made the Grand Tour of Europe; married 1837 Maria Louisa Brinckerhoff [dau of James Lefferts & Charlotte (Troup) Brinckerhoff] born c1817 NYC; they moved from NYC to the Espous property where he built a brick house in the then popular Tuscan Revival style of a Roman villa, described by one visitor as “Greek without and Roman within”. It was 70 feet square. The dining room, 28 feet long, was hung with pictures which were copies of masterpieces of Italian painting. Around this mansion lay the productive and landscaped acres of Pelham Farm, which was among the wonders of its day because of its orchards, its vineyards, its three fishponds, its wharves from which Pelham Farm apples were shipped to Europe and even to China. He is said to have had 25,000 apple trees. His specialty was the Yellow Newtown Pippin apple, a long-keeping apple largely exported and known in England by the time of the American Revolution. In the mid-1840s, Pell built a dock on the river with a large stone warehouse, where his apples were sweated to prepare them for their European journeys. It is said that they lasted nine months with this treatment. The Hudson By Daylight Map of 1878 shows the estate of Robert Pell on the west side of the Hudson south of Esopus Village. The map states that Pell’s farm was 1200 acres. His residence was located north of Esopus Village. The recession of 1857 hit Robert hard; and the Civil War ruined his apple export business. He was forced to sell his holdings. In 1869, he sold his holdings to his son-in-law, John B Ireland, who 35 years later sold a major portion to the Redemptorist Fathers (Mount St Alphonsus).

(3.11.6) REUBEN REYNOLDS – not mentioned in his father’s will – may have died early – relationship to the family is in question ********************************************************************* (3.1.1.3.1.4.10.6.6.2) Jane Lynn (Hornby) Fox AI grew up in Welch, WVA. At our home at 403 Stewart St. We moved there when I was about 5 years old from a house on Riverside Drive in Welch. The subject of genealogy was occasionally discussed and my father took us on trips to Basking Ridge NJ and to Cross River NY to visit his family. Genealogy began in earnest for me in January, 1991. We had discovered that Grady, our third grandson, was severely hearing impaired. I decided to find out if any others in our families had been similarly afflicted. Nothing came to light, but by that time I was hooked. At an early age I was interested in singing. My mother was the director of our youth choir at the Anderson Memorial Chapel. Naturally her children were in the choir, but I took it very seriously and learned from her some basic techniques in voice. Later we started attending the First Presbyterian Church downtown. There, the choir director was an accomplished and trained soprano. Her further instruction only increased my interest in singing. By the time I got to High School, I had already been singing for several years and had often been given soloist roles in the church choirs. In High School, I was a member of the A Cappella Choir for four years and took private singing lessons from the School choir director, Mrs. Stevenson. By this time I was singing in the Presbyterian Church Senior Choir. In school, I was usually selected to sing the solo parts and occasionally took a solo part in the church choir. High School was a very positive time for me. I was a member of every extra curricular club I could possibly join. I was, at the time, a member of the Future Teachers of America and the Future Nurses of America. These were mainly things to do during the lunch period, but my membership in the Thespians was very serious. I was just in about every play that the school produced during my high school years. If I wasn=t in one, it was only because I was already in another. I often had the lead roles. In the polling at the end of our senior year, I was voted, along with three other of my classmates, most talented. In 1958, I represented the local Garden Club as an attendant to the Welch, WVA Bi-Centennial Celebration. Also that year, I represented Welch High School in the West Virginia State Chorus. After graduation our sorority took a trip to Myrtle Beach SC, for one week and in early June, 1958, I went with my sister, Barbara, to Lakeville, Connecticut to a job at a summer resort there, the Interlaken (pronounced >Interlocken=) Inn. The Inn was owned and operated by the Peters= brothers, one of whom was Leon Peters, the President of Cushman, Wakefield, a large real estate company in New York City. Leon later became the owner of a racehorse, ACosta del Sol@, that won the Kentucky Derby around 1985. The Peters= family spent summers at Manaloking Beach in New Jersey. There, they met the Fox family who also summered there. Through them, Richard obtained a summer job at Interlaken Inn in 1958. He arrived at the Inn about June 15th. We met, dated, got pinned and then went back to our respective homes when school began for us both inn early September. We wrote throughout the year and saw each other only once - at Christmas time when Richard came to Welch for a visit. The next summer, I returned to Lakeville to work again at the Inn. Richard had, in the meantime, decided to work for the summer at Macy=s Department store near his home. His father was a buyer at the main store in New York City. Every weekend Richard traveled to Connecticut to see me. In July, he bought me a ring and asked me to marry him. I said yes. That September we again went back to school, Rich as a senior at Brown University, me as a sophomore at Marshall University in Huntington, WVA. In October of that year, as a late birthday present, Richard came to see me. We had been discussing our wedding plans by phone and letter for the past month. My parents were against a marriage at any time in the near future. In fact the admonition was that not only should Richard and I finish school, but Richard should finish graduate school and have a job in order to support me and any family which was sure to come along. To us this was ages away. We decided that week on October 20th, 1959, not to wait. We bought plane tickets and left for New York City. Six days later, on October 26, 1959 we were married in Millerton, New York, not far from Lakeville, Connecticut where we first met. Bill Peters and his wife, Rowena, stood up for us. Richard=s best friend and classmate at Brown, AGabby@ Hayes was his best man. I had just turned age 19. Ten months later, on August 25th, 1960, our son, Richard Frederick Fox born. I was a month and 11 days short of my 20th birthday. My singing career continued when we moved to Saxton=s River in 1961 when I joined the Congregational Church Choir, joined a local Strings Orchestra and became a member of the Marlboro Music Festival at Marlboro College, Marlboro, Vermont. The orchestra and chorus was directed by Blanch Moyse (pronounced Moe-ese=) a colleague of Rudolf Serkin and the wife of Marcel Moyse, all co-founders of the Marlboro Music Festival along with Adolf Busch and his brother, Herman. Many of the performances of the Marlboro Music Festival are still available on records and some are available on Compact Discs. I remained with the Chorus until 1968 when we moved to Plainfield NH. There I joined the many choruses in Hanover NH include the Handle Society. Singing, acting in local Thespians groups, volunteering my time in the church, Boy Scouts and Girl Scouts, along with raising my family became my career. I worked part-time selling Avon Products; as a desk clerk at the Old Grafton Tavern, Grafton VT; receptionist for the William Jewett Tucker Foundation at Dartmouth College, and Volunteer Coordinator for the Upper Valley R.S.V.P. program in Lebanon NH. In 1976 these activities came to an end when we moved to Gambier, Ohio where I was most unhappy. After a year we moved back to the east coast and I took a full time job as Securities Cashier at Cummings and Co., a brokerage firm in Providence, RI. One year later we moved to Groton, Massachusetts. I continued my job in Providence for a time, but later went to work as the Secretary to the Headmaster at Applewild School in Fitchburg MA. The job was overwhelming and I left to sell Life Insurance with John Hancock. I later learned that the school hired 3 people to replace my position and they now have five people doing the various jobs that I was responsible for. Selling life insurance was not my favorite job either. I learned a lot about ripping people off, especially the old and/or poor. My last job was as a researcher with the Development Office at Groton School. That job lasted until Richard went on his Sabbatical in 1989. In January, 1992, after we learned that Grady was deaf, I decided to pursue Genealogy. Genealogy continues to be my chosen career. And while it brings no salary, it brings great satisfaction. I have written a few articles for the Reynolds Family Association=s publication, AReynolds Recollections@. Another article, written this past spring for the New England Historic Genealogical Society, was not published. I learned from them that one of my principal sources, Anjou Gustave, is well-known for his fraudulent genealogies. On December 7, 1994, I was admitted as a member of the D.A.R. under Lt. Nathaniel Reynolds of New York. At the present time I am a member of the White Mountains Chapter in Littleton NH. My national number is 762340 and elected as Chapter Regent, Fall of 1995. His office has the reputation of being the most professional, the most effective, the most dedicated, and the hardest working office at the School. Rich=s accomplishments in Educational Fundraising, as well as at the World Wildlife Foundation, are recognized nationwide, and to some degree, worldwide. He is now listed in AWho=s Who in the East@ and in AWho=s Who in Education@. In 1989 he became the fundraising consultant to the World Wildlife Fund and Conservation Foundation and in 1990 a WWF National Council Member. He is a member of the Planned Giving Group of New England. He has contributed articles to CASE conference publications and other professional journals in the field of fund raising. He has been a consultant for development offices in other not-for-profit schools and organizations. While he works out of Groton MA, his home is Lancaster. He likes to travel and has been to Africa, France, England, Canada and most of the 49 states. His travels in 1995 took him again to England and then to Greece. The history of this cemetery is provided below: The Mount Carmel Cemetery is located on Kenwood Road in Sycamore Township, Hamilton County, Ohio. The cemetery and the Mount Carmel Baptist Church that once stood in the middle of it were established in 1823 on an acre of land donated to the church by Adam Lee, a church member and trustee. Lee was an early settler of Sycamore Township who purchased the entire section, 640 acres, in 1797 from the original patent holder, John Cleves Symmes (Hamilton County Deed Book B2:35). Mount Carmel Baptist Church was organized on July 20, 1822, with a moderator, a clerk and twenty-five original members including John, Isaac, and Enoch Ferris, Matthias Felter and Adam Lee. The Mount Carmel congregation grew rapidly, out-numbering the much earlier Carpenter=s Run Baptist congregation at the Blue Ash Meeting House which had been founded in 1797. The Carpenter=s Run society dissolved in 1828 after repeated internal disputes (Nelson and Runk 1894:453). An 1881 source averred that the AMount Carmel Church, whose meetinghouse is not far south of the Carpenter=s Run site, long since superseded the pioneer society@ (Ford and Ford 1881:391). Less than a year after the formation of the Mount Carmel Baptist society, member Adam Lee conveyed a one-acre parcel to the Trustees of the Mount Carmel Church, who were Andrew Hammel, John Ferris, and Adam Lee. On March 15, 1823, Lee stipulated in the indenture that if the one-acre parcel was not used as a Aplace of public worship and religious education or a burying ground,@ the agreement of conveyance would be void (Hamilton County Deed Book 23:9). Lee provided a clear impetus in his deed of gift for the new congregation to build a sanctuary and establish a cemetery immediately, and it is likely that they did. The church building was brick, and a late nineteenth century plat, which included a small sketch of the church, showed a gable front building facing east towards modern Kenwood Road (Nelson 1894:453 and Hamilton County Plat Book 9:19). A 1934 pen and ink drawing of the church by local artist Caroline Williams depicts a single story gable front brick building with two front doors and four windows along the sides of the small structure (Williams 1939:182, figure 2). The orientation of the building facing Kenwood Road indicates that the structure was built after the creation of the road. Indeed, this portion of Kenwood Road, first called Columbia Road, and later Madison or Madisonville Road, was established by the Hamilton County Commissioners on August 29, 1820, at least three years before the construction of the church (Hamilton County Road Records, Volume 2:88). It served as a major route from the northeastern part of the county to the Ohio River at the village of Columbia, located at the mouth of the Little Miami River. Evidence that the cemetery portion of Lee=s gift was put into immediate effect is in the early marked burial of one of the founding member=s children. Cronymous Felter, the twelve year old son of Matthias and Rebecca Felter, died on August 27, 1823, and was buried in the west central part of the graveyard; his is the earliest marked grave in the cemetery. His mother, Rebecca, died in 1830 and was buried near him. Both the church and the cemetery remained active at this site until 1915 when the congregation moved to a new church on Blue Ash Road. In 1941, the Board of Trustees of the Mount Carmel Church Cemetery elected to transfer the title of the cemetery property and the responsibility for its maintenance to the Trustees of Sycamore Township (Hamilton County Deed Book 1951:536). The name was officially changed at the dedication ceremony, November 24, 1991. ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------Earhart Genealogy provided by Myron B.

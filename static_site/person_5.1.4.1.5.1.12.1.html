CHARLES DAVENPORT LOCKWOOD

ID: 5.1.4.1.5.1.12.1 | Source: James
(5.1.4.1.5.1.12.1) CHARLES DAVENPORT LOCKWOOD (Photo), the only child of Henry and Helen Lockwood, born Stamford November 11, 1877 and died there December 6, 1949; received his early education in his native town and attended high school there graduating with Class of 1897; attended Sheffield Scientific School at Yale University, graduated in the class of 1900, Ph.B degree; and from the Law School of that institution 1903, LL.B.; while studying law, captained the basketball team, was editor of the Yale Law School Journal for 3y and represented Yale in debates against Harvard and Princeton; admitted to the bars of Connecticut 1903 and New York 1904; Assistant District Attorney in New York City under William T. Jerome, 1904-06; 1907 elected Judge of the Probate Court for the District of Stamford and was re-elected in 1909 and again in 1910; 1913 declined another term. In 1909 he formed a partnership with Homer S Cummings (Cummings & Lockwood law firm) in Stamford, which grew to about 170 attorneys. Charles represented Stamford in the lower house of the State Legislature in 1913 and ran as a candidate for Lt Governor in 1918; 1916 he was a delegate-at-large from Connecticut in the Democratic National Convention in St Louis and In San Francisco in 1920; also served as a member of the State Board of Finance and Control and as a State’s Attorney 1914; Chairman Stamford Exemption Board Council of Defense, World War I; Delegate-at-large to the Connecticut Constitution Convention 1933; Presidential elector-at-large for Roosevelt and Garner; Chairman of the Electoral College in Hartford; Director of the Citizens Savings Bank; Trustee of the Stamford’s Children's Home, director of the First-Stamford National Bank and Trust Co; the Stamford Morris Plan Bank; as well as president of the Shippan Point Land Company and secretary of the Lockwood and Palmer Co, his father’s hardware company where he worked as a boy and young man; director of the Stamford Water Co and the Blickensderfer Typerwriter Co; and Trustee of the Society of the Presbyterian Church. Homer S Cummings resigned from the firm in 1933 to become Attorney General of the U.S. and when Charles died, Cummings said of him, “I look back upon a period of more than forty years and find no fault with him, forty years without a disagreement or even a word of fleeting irritation.” Lockwood, Charles Davenport. Story Of Stamford - Historical Address -On the occasion of the celebration of the 275th anniversary of the founding of the Town of Stamford, Connecticut - Delivered in the Stamford Theatre on June Eleventh, 1916. (Stamford, Connecticut); 1916. 29 pp., paper covers, 23 cm. Location: CtS, CtSHi. Parks (No. 8582). "This meeting today is particularly a Stamford congregation, and we are here primarily to congratulate ourselves over the proud history of our town, to pay a tribute to those who have labored in times past for the development of Stamford, and perhaps to consider whether we are in a proper way upholding and continuing the work so nobly instituted and carried on by those who are with us no longer, and whose monument is the town in which we dwell...Within the next twenty-five years many questions and reforms should receive the attention of our citizens. All admit that in our town and city governments with a duplicate set of officials we have a cumbersome, ineffective and expensive system of local government...Who will change the system? If the partners in this municipal enterprise will live up to their obligations, improvements in all these directions will come not in a day or a year, for it takes a generation for an innovation to become a custom, but this does not excuse us from making a beginning." Charles Davenport Lockwood, pp. 3, 27-28. He married October 13, 1906 Gertrude Bell [dau of Harry & Olive E. (Barber) Bell] (1880-), and born to them were:
